% CASE A1 — weak graph/evidence support + output collapse
\textbf{(A) Stable wrong answer with weak graph support} &
\emph{Are TEC-1 and Dubna 48K based on the same processor?} &
Gold: \emph{yes}.
KG: \emph{no}~(\textbf{\texttimes}).
Vanilla: \emph{yes}~(\checkmark). &
All five KG samples repeat the wrong answer
($\mathrm{DSE}=0$, SD-UQ$=0.006$), so output-only confidence looks
low-risk.
Here the warning is evidence-facing and graph-facing rather than answer-facing:
GPS sits at the fallback value $0.5$, and SEU rises to $0.75$, indicating weak
local evidence support for the repeated answer.
This is the clean case for the narrower claim:
answer agreement can look low-risk even though the retrieved graph state and
evidence support do not. \\
\midrule
%
% CASE A2 — wrong but locally coherent graph path
\textbf{(B) Wrong but graph-coherent} &
\emph{Which country refrained from participating in the 1991 Baltic Cup though it had participated in previous Baltic Cup competitions?} &
Gold: \emph{Belarus}.
KG: \emph{Estonia}~(\textbf{\texttimes}).
Vanilla: \emph{Belarus}~(\checkmark). &
This case is useful for the opposite reason.
KG-RAG is still wrong with low answer variation
($\mathrm{DSE}=0$, SD-UQ$\approx 0$), but GPS is $0.0$ rather than an
abstention because the retrieved graph supports a locally coherent, wrong
entity neighbourhood.
GPS is therefore behaving as a graph-consistency score, not failing as a
correctness test.
The lesson is conditional utility:
sometimes GPS abstains and helps, and sometimes a wrong anchor looks
structurally coherent enough that scalar scores alone are insufficient and
the qualitative provenance trace has to be inspected. \\
